01 — The Opportunity

You're building something Vegas needs

Anyone who has tried to plan a party in Las Vegas knows how scattered the process is. You ask friends for referrals, dig through Facebook groups, scroll Yelp hoping someone has a review, and still end up with half a vendor list and no idea who to trust. Meanwhile, the taco truck owner, the DJ, the balloon artist are spending their time posting in Facebook groups and hoping someone sees it.

Party Local fixes both sides of that problem. It gives clients one place to find, compare, and connect with local event vendors. And it gives mom-and-pop vendors a real home on the web, not just a buried listing on a platform that rewards whoever pays the most.

The platforms people use today like The Knot, WeddingWire, and Thumbtack are built for national reach and charge vendors to be seen. Party Local is built for your neighborhood first, with community at the center and fair access for small businesses from day one.

02 — Research and Discovery

Copy and strategy built on real pain points

Before a single line of copy or code was written, research was done into the real frustrations that both sides of this marketplace experience. Reddit threads, community forums, and competitor reviews were reviewed to understand what people actually say when no one is selling to them.

This research directly shapes the copy, the feature priorities, and the way each page speaks to its audience. Every word on the Party Local site is grounded in something a real person actually said or felt.

Client pain point

"I spent two weeks just finding vendors"

Most clients piece together their vendor list through word of mouth and scattered social posts. There is no central place to discover, compare, and connect with local party vendors.

Client pain point

"The big platforms hide the real prices"

Rankings on The Knot and WeddingWire are influenced by vendor spend. What gets labeled "budget" is not affordable for real families, and honest pricing is nearly impossible to find.

Vendor pain point

"We post on Facebook and go unnoticed"

Small vendors without marketing budgets struggle to get in front of the right clients. Lead-gen platforms charge per inquiry, eating into margins for businesses just getting started.

Vendor pain point

"Finding events to pitch is a full-time job"

Vendors have no reliable way to find upcoming events where they could offer their services. Word of mouth and cold outreach are the only options, and neither one scales.

Research conducted March 2026 across Reddit, Quora, WeddingPro, and competitor review data. Findings used to shape all copy, feature priorities, and user flows in this build.

06 — Search and AI Visibility

Getting found on Google and in AI

Launching a platform without a visibility strategy means competing on word of mouth alone. Party Local needs to show up when someone in Las Vegas searches "taco truck for a quinceanera" or asks ChatGPT "where do I find a DJ for a birthday party in Vegas." That takes three layers of optimization working together from day one.

SEOTraditional Search

Google rankings

Classic keyword-based optimization that helps Party Local rank in search results when people search for local vendors, event planning help, or specific categories like "Las Vegas wedding florist." Includes on-page structure, metadata, internal linking, and local SEO signals so Las Vegas shows up as the primary market.

  • Keyword research for vendor categories and event types
  • Page titles, meta descriptions, and header structure
  • Local SEO setup (Google Business Profile signals, city pages)
  • Site speed and mobile performance as ranking factors
AIOAI Overviews

Google AI Overview extraction

Google now answers many search queries directly at the top of results using an AI-generated summary pulled from trusted pages. Getting Party Local into those summaries means writing content in a specific format: clear questions and direct answers, structured sections, and authoritative language that AI can pull from confidently.

  • FAQ-style content written for AI extraction
  • Clear answer formatting so Google pulls Party Local content
  • Blog and resource structure designed to feed AI summaries
  • Ongoing content updates tied to high-AIO-volume queries
GEOGenerative Engine Optimization

AI chatbot citations

When someone asks ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, or Google Gemini for vendor recommendations in Las Vegas, the answer they get is pulled from structured data and authoritative pages across the web. Schema markup tells AI systems exactly what Party Local is, where it operates, and what it offers, so it gets cited as a local resource.

  • JSON-LD schema for LocalBusiness, VendorListing, and EventService
  • Structured data markup for vendor profiles and categories
  • Entity signals that connect Party Local to Las Vegas event planning
  • Citation-friendly content that AI chatbots can reference

Included in the $7,500 build: Initial SEO setup, site-wide schema markup, Google Business Profile configuration, AIO-ready content structure, and GEO entity signals. This is baked into the build, not an add-on. Ongoing optimization and content updates are included in the Growth and Scale maintenance tiers.

01
The live demo is the pitch

Pull up the Party Local prototype on a phone or tablet and let vendors browse it. Showing is stronger than explaining. Let them find their own vendor category and see a sample profile in 60 seconds without you saying much. The demo replaces the slide deck.

02
One QR code, one action

Print a small card (business card size) with a single QR code linking to the waitlist form. The form has a vendor path and a client path. Do not send people to the homepage or social accounts. One scan, one form. Aim for 30 cards per hour at the event.

Vendors who sign up at Derby in the Desert get a Founding Vendor badge on their profile, free featured placement for the first 60 days, and their name in the platform launch announcement. 04
Capture the room on social

Post a Story or short video during the event showing the demo and the crowd. Tag the event, use Las Vegas event hashtags. This creates social proof in real time and reaches vendors at the event who have not come over yet. The Instagram account needs to be live before May 2nd for this to work.

05
Follow up within 24 hours

Every vendor who joined the waitlist at Derby in the Desert gets a direct follow-up email the next morning referencing the event and the Founding Vendor offer with one clear next step. This is the difference between a warm conversation and a conversion.

Derby activation goal: 20 vendor waitlist signups on May 2nd. Anything above that is momentum heading into launch.

09 — Operations Playbook

Things to have a plan for before launch

The platform does not run itself after launch day. These are the operational decisions Party Local needs to make early, before they become problems. None require a large investment, but all require a decision and a named owner.

Vendor Supply Strategy

Vendors come before clients

A directory with no vendors is useless to anyone searching it. The goal before full launch is 30 or more active vendor profiles across at least five categories. Derby in the Desert is the first push. Direct outreach to vendors already active in the Party Local Facebook group is the second.

Recruit vendors first. Clients will come once there is something to find.

Community Moderation

Someone owns the forum

Spam, off-topic posts, and vendor disputes will appear within the first two weeks. A short community guidelines page and a report button on every post handles most of it passively. But a named moderator still needs to do a weekly review.

  • Weekly review of all new posts for the first 90 days
  • Community guidelines page linked in the footer
  • Report button on every forum thread
  • One named moderator, ideally one of the founders
Email Infrastructure

Where the list lives

The waitlist form will collect emails from day one. Those contacts need to land in an email platform with a welcome sequence ready to go, not a spreadsheet. Setup and a three-email welcome sequence for each user type is included in the Promote and Convert marketing tiers.

  • Recommendation: Mailchimp (free up to 500 contacts) or ConvertKit
  • Separate lists for vendors and clients from the start
  • Welcome email triggers within 5 minutes of signup
  • 3-email onboarding sequence for each side
Onboarding Sequences

What happens after signup

A vendor who creates an account and never completes their profile is a lost listing. A client who signs up and never posts an event is a lost lead. Automated onboarding emails nudge both sides toward the action that makes the platform useful.

  • Vendor: welcome, profile completion nudge on day 3, first inquiry tips on day 7
  • Client: welcome, how to post an event on day 1, vendor spotlight on day 5
  • Triggered by account creation, not a calendar blast
  • Included in Growth and Scale maintenance tiers

10 — How Party Local Makes Money

Monetization ideas for the platform

Party Local can stay free for browsers and basic users while generating revenue through a few well-placed upgrades. Here are the most realistic options for a startup platform building trust in a local market:

  • 01

    Featured Vendor Listings

    Vendors pay a small monthly fee to appear at the top of search results in their category. Disclosed to users so trust stays intact. Suggested range: $50–$150/month per vendor.

  • 02

    Verified Badge Subscription

    Vendors who complete their profile and collect reviews can pay for a Verified badge that builds client confidence. Suggested range: $25–$75/month.

  • 03

    Lead Alerts for Vendors

    Paid vendors get notified immediately when a matching event is posted. Free vendors see it later. Suggested range: $30–$60/month.

  • 04

    Booking Commission (Future Phase)

    Once booking is enabled through the platform, a small percentage fee per transaction generates revenue at scale without charging vendors upfront.

  • 05

    Sponsored Community Posts

    Vendors pay to have a promoted post appear in the community feed. Clearly labeled as sponsored. Suggested range: $50–$200 per post.
